Heart Rate Zone Training

Stop guessing. Train in the right zones for your goals.

Why Train with Heart Rate?

Your heart rate is the best real-time indicator of exercise intensity. Using it to guide training means you're not just working hard—you're working smart.

The Problem with "Feel"

Many people train too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days. Heart rate keeps you honest. Zone 2 should feel easy—if you can't hold a conversation, you're going too hard.

The 5 Heart Rate Zones

Zone 1: Recovery (50-60% Max HR)

Feel: Very easy. You could do this all day.

Purpose: Active recovery, warm-up, cool-down. Promotes blood flow without fatigue.

Zone 2: Aerobic Base (60-70% Max HR)

Feel: Comfortable. Can hold full conversation. "Easy" pace.

Purpose: Fat burning, building aerobic foundation. Most of your training should be here. Builds mitochondria, capillaries, and cardiac efficiency.

Zone 3: Tempo (70-80% Max HR)

Feel: "Comfortably hard." Can speak in sentences but prefer not to.

Purpose: Improves lactate threshold, increases sustainable pace. Use sparingly—easy to overtrain here.

Zone 4: Threshold (80-90% Max HR)

Feel: Hard. Can only say a few words at a time. Breathing is labored.

Purpose: Increases VO2 max, improves speed. Classic "interval" intensity. Effective but taxing.

Zone 5: Maximum (90-100% Max HR)

Feel: All-out. Cannot speak. Sustainable only for short bursts.

Purpose: Maximum power, sprint training. Very limited use for most people. High injury and burnout risk.

Calculating Your Zones

There are several methods to calculate your training zones:

Method 1: Percentage of Max Heart Rate

Most common formula: Max HR = 220 - Age

Better formula (Tanaka): Max HR = 208 - (0.7 × Age)

Method 2: Heart Rate Reserve (Karvonen Method)

More accurate, accounts for resting heart rate:

Target HR = ((Max HR - Resting HR) × % intensity) + Resting HR

This method personalizes zones to your current fitness level.

Method 3: Field Testing

The most accurate method: actually test your max heart rate with a field test. Warning: Only for those cleared for vigorous exercise.

The 80/20 Rule

Elite endurance athletes spend about 80% of training time in Zones 1-2 and only 20% in high-intensity Zones 4-5. Most recreational exercisers do the opposite—and that's a problem.

❌ Common Mistake

Going "medium hard" every session. Too hard to build aerobic base, too easy to improve speed. You get the worst of both worlds.

✅ Better Approach

Easy days truly easy (Zone 2), hard days truly hard (Zone 4-5). This "polarized" approach maximizes adaptation with minimum fatigue.

Zone 2: The Most Underrated Zone

If you only remember one thing from this page: Zone 2 training is powerful.

  • Builds the foundation for all other training
  • Improves fat metabolism (you become a better fat burner)
  • Increases mitochondrial density
  • Improves cardiac efficiency
  • Low injury risk, sustainable long-term
  • Can do more volume without burnout

The catch? It feels "too easy." That's the point. Trust the process.
