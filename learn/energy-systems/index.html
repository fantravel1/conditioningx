Energy Systems Explained

How your body fuels exercise. Understanding this changes how you train.

The Fuel Your Body Uses

Every movement you make requires energy. That energy comes from one molecule: ATP (adenosine triphosphate). Your body has three systems to produce ATP, and understanding them helps you train smarter.

System 1: ATP-PC (Phosphocreatine)

Duration: 0-10 seconds of maximal effort

Fuel source: Stored ATP and creatine phosphate in muscles

Examples: 100m sprint, vertical jump, 1-rep max lift, throwing a punch

Pros: Immediate, maximum power output, no "burn"

Cons: Depletes very quickly, needs 2-5 minutes to fully recover

This system is like a nitro boostâ€”instant power, but limited. You literally run out of stored ATP in about 10 seconds of all-out effort.

System 2: Glycolytic (Anaerobic)

Duration: 10 seconds to ~2 minutes of intense effort

Fuel source: Glucose (from blood sugar and muscle glycogen)

Examples: 400m sprint, hard interval repeats, circuit training rounds

Pros: Produces ATP quickly, can sustain high intensity

Cons: Produces lactate (the "burn"), fatigues relatively quickly

This system breaks down glucose without oxygen (hence "anaerobic"). It's fast but creates metabolic byproducts that cause that familiar burning sensation. You can't maintain this intensity for long.

System 3: Oxidative (Aerobic)

Duration: 2+ minutes, can sustain for hours

Fuel source: Carbohydrates AND fats (plus protein in extreme cases)

Examples: Jogging, cycling, swimming, hiking, Zone 2 cardio

Pros: Sustainable, can last for hours, uses fat for fuel

Cons: Lower power output, requires oxygen (hence breathing hard)

This is your endurance engine. It requires oxygen to produce ATP efficiently. The trade-off? It can't produce power as quickly as the other systems. But it can keep going for hours.

They Work Together

Here's the key insight: all three systems are always working. The question is which one is dominant.

Example: 800m Run (~2 minutes)

  • Start: ATP-PC fires for the explosive start
  • First 30-60 seconds: Glycolytic system takes over as you push hard
  • Final minute: Oxidative system contributes more as you try to maintain pace
  • Result: All three systems contribute, but in different proportions

Training Implications

Want More Endurance?

Train the oxidative system with Zone 2 work. Long, easy efforts. This builds mitochondria (your cellular power plants) and improves fat metabolism.

Want More Speed/Power?

Train the ATP-PC system with short, all-out sprints with full recovery. 10-second efforts with 2-3 minute rests.

Want Better Interval Performance?

Train the glycolytic system with hard intervals in the 30-second to 2-minute range. This is where HIIT lives.

Want Overall Conditioning?

Train all three systems, with emphasis on the oxidative system as your foundation. The 80/20 rule applies: 80% easy (oxidative), 20% hard (glycolytic/ATP-PC).
