The Centenarian Olympics: Training for Life at 100

Peter Attia's framework for training today to be active and capable in your final decades.

Longevity Framework Training

A Different Way to Think About Fitness

Most fitness goals are short-term: look good for summer, hit a PR, finish a race. Dr. Peter Attia, longevity physician and author of Outlive, proposes something radically different: train for what you want to be able to do at 100.

He calls this the "Centenarian Olympics" — a personal set of physical tasks you want to perform in your final decade of life. The framework reverses-engineers your training from that endpoint.

The Core Question

What do you want to be able to do when you're 90 or 100? Hike with grandchildren? Get up from the floor? Carry groceries? Your training today should be building toward that capacity — with margin to spare.

Why This Framework Matters

Physical capacity declines predictably with age:

  • VO2 max: ~10% decline per decade after 30
  • Muscle mass: 3-8% loss per decade after 30
  • Strength: Accelerating decline after 50
  • Power: Declines faster than strength
  • Balance: Deteriorates progressively

If you're barely functional at 50, you won't be functional at 80. The Centenarian Olympics framework asks: what baseline do you need today to maintain capacity for decades?

Sample Centenarian Olympic Events

These are Attia's examples. Yours will be personal:

Physical Tasks at 100

  • Pick up a small child from the floor (goblet squat, hinge pattern)
  • Get up from the floor without using hands (mobility, strength)
  • Carry groceries up stairs (loaded carries, leg strength)
  • Hike for an hour with 1000ft elevation (cardio, leg endurance)
  • Put a carry-on in the overhead bin (pressing, shoulder mobility)
  • Catch yourself if you trip (power, reaction time, balance)

Reverse Engineering Your Training

If you want to be able to do a goblet squat with 30 lbs at 100, you need to account for strength loss. Working backward:

  • At 100: Goblet squat 30 lbs
  • At 80: Need ~50 lbs (accounting for decline)
  • At 60: Need ~80 lbs
  • At 40: Need ~100+ lbs to have margin

This reveals whether your current training is adequate. Many people are already below the trajectory needed to maintain independence.

The Four Pillars

Attia identifies four fitness domains essential for longevity:

1. Stability

The foundation. Ability to safely transmit force without injuring yourself. Includes:

  • Core stability and anti-rotation
  • Foot and ankle function
  • Shoulder stability
  • Hip function
  • Breathing mechanics

2. Strength

Raw force production. Essential for independence and injury prevention:

  • Hip hinge (deadlift patterns)
  • Squat patterns
  • Upper body push and pull
  • Grip strength (highly correlated with longevity)
  • Loaded carries

3. Aerobic Efficiency (Zone 2)

Mitochondrial health and fat oxidation:

  • 3-4+ hours per week of Zone 2 cardio
  • Builds the aerobic base
  • Improves metabolic health
  • Low injury risk, sustainable long-term

4. Anaerobic Performance (VO2 Max)

Peak cardiovascular output:

  • 1-2 high-intensity sessions per week
  • VO2 max is the strongest longevity predictor
  • Maintains peak capacity as the ceiling for daily function

Designing Your Events

Think about your values and lifestyle. What activities define quality of life for you?

Questions to Ask

  • What physical activities bring you joy now?
  • What do you want to do with grandchildren?
  • What tasks would represent independence?
  • What would make you feel vital and capable at 90?

Your Centenarian Olympics might include:

  • Play 18 holes of golf walking
  • Swim laps for 30 minutes
  • Tend a garden (kneeling, getting up, carrying)
  • Dance at a wedding
  • Travel independently

Practical Implementation

Weekly Framework

  • Zone 2 cardio: 3-4 sessions, 45-60 min each
  • Strength training: 3 sessions, full body or upper/lower split
  • High-intensity cardio: 1-2 sessions
  • Stability/mobility: Daily, 10-15 min

Starting Points by Decade

  • 30s: Build a strong base. You have time — use it to develop robust capacity.
  • 40s: Critical decade. Prioritize consistency and injury prevention.
  • 50s: Focus on maintaining strength and VO2 max. Protein intake becomes crucial.
  • 60s: Power and balance training become essential. Every bit of muscle matters.
  • 70+: It's never too late. Even modest training produces significant benefits.

The Bottom Line

The Centenarian Olympics framework shifts fitness from vanity to functionality, from short-term to lifetime. Instead of training for aesthetics or arbitrary PRs, you're building the physical reserve to live fully in your final decades.

Define your events. Work backward to what you need today. Train consistently for decades. The goal isn't to be the fittest person in the gym — it's to be doing goblet squats with your great-grandchildren.
