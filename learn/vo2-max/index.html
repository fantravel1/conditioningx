VO2 Max Explained

The single best predictor of cardiovascular health and longevity. What it is and how to improve it.

What is VO2 Max?

VO2 max is the maximum amount of oxygen your body can use during intense exercise. It's measured in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min). The higher your VO2 max, the more fit you are.

Why It Matters

Research shows VO2 max is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. Low VO2 max is associated with higher risk of heart disease, diabetes, and early death. Improving your VO2 max literally extends your life.

VO2 Max Ranges

Poor

< 35

ml/kg/min (typical for sedentary adults)

Good

40-50

ml/kg/min (regular exercisers)

Elite

60+

ml/kg/min (endurance athletes)

How to Test Your VO2 Max

Lab Testing (Gold Standard)

The most accurate method involves running on a treadmill or cycling while wearing a mask that measures oxygen consumption. Intensity increases until exhaustion.

Field Tests

  • Cooper Test: Run as far as possible in 12 minutes. Distance correlates to VO2 max.
  • Beep Test: Shuttle runs with increasing speed until failure.
  • 1.5 Mile Run: Time a 1.5 mile run. Faster times = higher VO2 max.

Wearables

Many fitness watches (Garmin, Apple Watch, WHOOP) estimate VO2 max based on heart rate data during exercise. These are approximations but useful for tracking trends.

How to Improve VO2 Max

The good news: VO2 max is highly trainable. Even older adults can significantly improve their VO2 max with proper training.

1. Build Your Base First

Spend 4-8 weeks doing mostly Zone 2 training before adding high-intensity work. This builds the aerobic foundation that supports VO2 max improvements.

2. Add High-Intensity Intervals

Once you have a base, add 1-2 VO2 max-specific workouts per week:

  • Long intervals: 3-5 min at 90-95% max HR with equal rest
  • Short intervals: 30 sec all-out, 30 sec rest, 10-15 repeats
  • Hill repeats: Sprint up hills for 2-3 minutes, walk down

3. Maintain Consistency

VO2 max improvements take time. Expect 5-15% improvement over 8-12 weeks of consistent training.

Sample Week for VO2 Max Improvement

  • Monday: Zone 2 easy run (45 min)
  • Tuesday: Rest or light activity
  • Wednesday: VO2 max intervals (5 × 3 min hard, 3 min easy)
  • Thursday: Zone 2 cross-training (cycling, swimming)
  • Friday: Rest
  • Saturday: Long Zone 2 run (60-90 min)
  • Sunday: Active recovery

Age and VO2 Max

VO2 max naturally declines with age—about 1% per year after 30. But regular training can slow this decline dramatically. A fit 60-year-old can have a higher VO2 max than a sedentary 30-year-old.
