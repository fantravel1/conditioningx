FRC and Joint CARs: The Complete Guide to Joint Health

How elite athletes and movement specialists are revolutionizing joint longevity through Functional Range Conditioning.

Mobility FRC Joint Health

What Is FRC?

Functional Range Conditioning (FRC) is a comprehensive joint training system developed by Dr. Andreo Spina. It's built on a simple but powerful premise: you should have control over your body's full range of motion.

FRC distinguishes between flexibility (passive range of motion) and mobility (active, controlled range). Many people can be stretched into positions they can't actively achieve or control — this is a recipe for injury.

The Core Principle

Flexibility without strength is useless. FRC trains you to own your ranges — to have strength, control, and neurological awareness at every point in your joint's available motion.

Why Joint Health Matters

Your joints are the foundation of all movement. Without healthy, mobile joints:

  • Movement compensations develop around restrictions
  • Force transmission becomes inefficient
  • Injury risk increases at end ranges
  • Chronic pain patterns emerge
  • Athletic performance suffers

Joints that don't move through full range progressively lose that range. The body follows a "use it or lose it" principle. FRC is systematic joint maintenance.

The FRC Framework

1. Controlled Articular Rotations (CARs)

The foundation of FRC. CARs are active, controlled circular movements that take a joint through its full range of motion while maintaining tension in surrounding tissues.

Key principles:

  • Full range: Move through every available degree of motion
  • Irradiation: Create tension in surrounding joints to isolate the target
  • Slow and controlled: No momentum, pure muscular control
  • Both directions: Circles clockwise and counterclockwise

2. Progressive and Regressive Angular Isometric Loading (PAILs/RAILs)

The expansion method. PAILs and RAILs build strength at end ranges to expand usable mobility:

  • PAILs: Contract into the stretching tissue (trying to close the stretch)
  • RAILs: Contract the opposite tissue (trying to deepen the stretch)

3. Kinetic Stretching (Holds and Lifts)

Active end-range training. Hold positions at end range using only muscular contraction, no external support. This builds control where most injuries happen.

CARs: The Daily Practice

CARs should be done daily — they're joint maintenance, like brushing your teeth. A full-body CARs routine takes 10-15 minutes.

Full-Body CARs Routine

  1. Neck: Slow, controlled circles (30 sec each direction)
  2. Shoulders: Large, controlled circles (30 sec each arm, each direction)
  3. Scapulae: Protraction, elevation, retraction, depression (8-10 cycles)
  4. Wrists: Full circles with tension (20 sec each direction)
  5. Spine: Segmental flexion/extension, rotation (60 sec total)
  6. Hips: Standing hip circles (30 sec each leg, each direction)
  7. Knees: Flexion/extension with rotation (20 sec each)
  8. Ankles: Full circles (20 sec each direction)

Do this every morning. Your joints will thank you.

How to Perform CARs Correctly

Hip CARs (Example)

  1. Stand on one leg, holding something for balance if needed
  2. Create total body tension — squeeze glute of standing leg, brace core
  3. Lift knee as high as possible while maintaining posture
  4. Rotate knee out to the side (external rotation)
  5. Extend leg behind you without arching back
  6. Continue circle back to start position
  7. Move as slowly as possible — 30 seconds per circle minimum
  8. Reverse direction

The Irradiation Principle

When performing CARs, create tension in all surrounding joints. For hip CARs, squeeze your core, clench your fists, and create full-body tension. This isolates the working joint and increases neurological drive.

PAILs/RAILs Protocol

This is how you expand range of motion with FRC — not by stretching more, but by building strength at end range.

PAILs/RAILs Sequence

  1. Passive Stretch: Hold end-range position for 2 minutes to create tissue adaptation
  2. PAILs: Contract into the stretch (try to close it) at increasing intensity — 30 sec ramping to max, hold max 10-20 sec
  3. RAILs: Immediately contract the opposite direction (try to deepen the stretch) — ramp up and hold 10-20 sec
  4. New Range: Settle into the new end range. You should be slightly deeper.
  5. Repeat 2-3 cycles

Example: Hip 90/90 PAILs/RAILs

  • Sit in 90/90 position (front leg 90° flexion, back leg 90° internal rotation)
  • Hinge forward to end range, hold 2 minutes
  • PAILs: Press front shin into floor hard (as if trying to close the hip), ramp up over 30 sec
  • RAILs: Try to lift knee off floor using hip muscles only (as if trying to deepen the stretch)
  • Settle into new range, repeat

Who Should Do FRC?

Everyone. But these groups benefit especially:

Ideal Candidates

  • Athletes: Bulletproof joints for sport demands
  • Desk workers: Counter the effects of prolonged sitting
  • Aging adults: Maintain joint health and independence
  • Post-rehab: Restore function after injury
  • Movement practitioners: Yoga teachers, martial artists, dancers
  • Anyone with restrictions: Systematic improvement of limited joints

FRC vs. Traditional Stretching

Aspect Traditional Stretching FRC
Focus Flexibility (passive) Mobility (active control)
Method Lengthen tissue Build strength at end range
Results Temporary increase in ROM Permanent, controlled ROM
Injury Risk Can create unstable hypermobility Creates stable, strong ranges

Sample Weekly FRC Schedule

  • Daily: Full-body CARs (10-15 min, morning)
  • Mon/Thu: Hip PAILs/RAILs (20 min)
  • Tue/Fri: Shoulder PAILs/RAILs (20 min)
  • Wed/Sat: Spine and ankle work (15 min)
  • Pre-workout: Sport-specific CARs for joints being loaded

Common Mistakes

  • Moving too fast: CARs should be painfully slow. If you can do a full hip CAR in under 20 seconds, you're rushing.
  • Not enough tension: The irradiation creates neurological drive. Without tension, you're just moving through space.
  • Skipping the passive hold: PAILs/RAILs require the 2-minute passive stretch first for tissue adaptation.
  • Expecting overnight results: Joint adaptation takes weeks to months. Consistency beats intensity.

Getting Started

  1. Week 1-2: Learn and practice full-body CARs daily
  2. Week 3-4: Add PAILs/RAILs for one restricted joint
  3. Week 5+: Expand PAILs/RAILs to additional joints as needed
  4. Ongoing: CARs become non-negotiable daily practice

Bottom Line

FRC and CARs represent a paradigm shift in how we think about mobility. Instead of passively stretching, you actively build control. Instead of hoping flexibility transfers to function, you train the function directly.

Start with CARs tomorrow morning. Ten minutes. Every joint in your body. Do this for a month and you'll understand why elite athletes and movement specialists are obsessed with this system.
