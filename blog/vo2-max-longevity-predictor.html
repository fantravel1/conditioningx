VO2 Max as a Longevity Predictor: How to Train It at Any Age

Cardiorespiratory fitness is the strongest predictor of mortality. Here's how to improve yours.

Longevity VO2 Max Performance

The Most Important Number for Longevity

If you could only know one number about your health, it should be your VO2 max. This single metric — your maximal oxygen uptake capacity — is the strongest predictor of all-cause mortality we have, outperforming smoking status, diabetes, and hypertension.

A landmark 2018 JAMA study following 122,007 patients found that cardiorespiratory fitness was inversely associated with mortality with no upper limit of benefit. Translation: the fitter you are, the longer you're likely to live — and there's no point where being more fit stops helping.

The Data

Moving from the bottom 25% to the top 25% of cardiorespiratory fitness is associated with a 5x reduction in mortality risk. This effect is larger than quitting smoking.

What Is VO2 Max?

VO2 max measures the maximum amount of oxygen your body can use during intense exercise. It's expressed as milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min).

This number reflects the integrated function of your:

  • Heart: How much blood it can pump
  • Lungs: How efficiently they oxygenate blood
  • Blood vessels: How well they deliver oxygen
  • Muscles: How effectively they use oxygen

What's a Good VO2 Max?

VO2 Max Reference Values (ml/kg/min)

Category Men (20-39) Women (20-39)
Poor <35 <28
Fair 35-40 28-33
Good 41-47 34-40
Excellent 48-55 41-47
Superior >55 >47

Values decrease approximately 10% per decade after age 30. Elite endurance athletes often exceed 70-85 ml/kg/min.

The Longevity Target

Dr. Peter Attia suggests aiming for the top quartile for your age — or even the top quartile of the next decade younger. Why?

  • VO2 max naturally declines ~10% per decade
  • At 80, you want to still be functional
  • Building a "reserve" now protects future capability

Practical target: Aim for a VO2 max that would be "good" for someone 20 years younger than you.

How to Measure Your VO2 Max

Gold Standard: Lab Testing

A graded exercise test (GXT) with respiratory gas analysis. Most accurate but requires specialized equipment and costs $150-300.

Accessible Estimates

  • Cooper 12-Minute Test: Run as far as possible in 12 minutes. Use our calculator
  • Rockport Walk Test: Walk 1 mile as fast as possible, record time and heart rate
  • Smartwatch estimates: Apple Watch, Garmin, WHOOP provide estimates from training data

How to Improve VO2 Max

VO2 max is trainable at any age, though the rate of improvement varies:

  • Untrained individuals: Can improve 15-30% in 12-20 weeks
  • Moderately trained: 5-15% improvement possible
  • Highly trained: 1-3% gains are significant

The Two-Pronged Approach

Zone 2 Training (80%)

Build the aerobic base. 3-4+ hours per week at conversational pace. Improves mitochondrial density and fat oxidation.

High-Intensity (20%)

Push the ceiling. 1-2 sessions per week of intervals at 90-100% max HR. Directly challenges VO2 max.

Best High-Intensity Protocols for VO2 Max

4x4 Norwegian Protocol

  • 4 minutes at 90-95% max HR
  • 3-4 minutes active recovery at 70% max HR
  • Repeat 4 times
  • Total: ~30-35 minutes including warm-up/cool-down

Research shows this protocol maximizes time spent at VO2 max.

Tabata-Style Intervals

  • 20 seconds all-out effort
  • 10 seconds rest
  • Repeat 8 times (4 minutes)
  • Can repeat 2-3 sets with longer rest between

Time-efficient but extremely demanding. Not for beginners.

Training VO2 Max After 40

Good news: VO2 max responds to training at any age. Key considerations for older athletes:

  • Recovery: Allow more time between high-intensity sessions (2-3 days)
  • Ramp-up: Progress intensity more gradually
  • Consistency: Years of steady training beat short intense bursts
  • Strength: Maintain muscle mass — it affects VO2 max calculation and functional capacity

Sample Weekly Schedule

Balanced Week for VO2 Max Improvement

  • Monday: Zone 2 — 45 min easy cycling or walking
  • Tuesday: Strength training
  • Wednesday: Zone 2 — 45 min + short strides (4x20 sec)
  • Thursday: High-intensity — 4x4 intervals
  • Friday: Rest or light mobility
  • Saturday: Long Zone 2 — 60-90 min
  • Sunday: Active recovery or rest

The Bottom Line

VO2 max is the single best biomarker for longevity we have. Unlike many health markers, it's highly trainable — even into your 70s and beyond. The investment in cardiorespiratory fitness pays dividends not just in performance, but in years and quality of life.

Start by measuring or estimating your current VO2 max. Then build a program with plenty of Zone 2 work supplemented by strategic high-intensity intervals. In 12 weeks, test again. The numbers will move.
