Soccer: Building a 90-Minute Engine

How elite players cover 10+ kilometers while maintaining sprint ability through injury time. The science of soccer endurance.

Soccer Endurance Conditioning

The Physical Demands of Soccer

Soccer is one of the most demanding sports on the planet. Players must sustain activity for 90+ minutes while being ready to sprint at any moment. The game waits for no one — you either have the fitness or you don't.

GPS data from professional matches reveals:

  • Total Distance: 10-13 km per game (6-8 miles)
  • High-Intensity Running: 800-1,200m above 19.8 km/h
  • Sprints: 200-400m at max speed (30-60 sprints per game)
  • Changes of Direction: 700-1,000+ per game
  • Accelerations/Decelerations: 100-150 significant efforts

The Key Insight

Soccer fitness isn't just about running a lot. It's about maintaining the ability to sprint, change direction, and make explosive movements in minute 89 — when the game is on the line.

The Two Engines

Soccer requires two distinct physiological capacities that must work together:

Engine 1: The Aerobic Base

This is your ability to sustain work for 90 minutes. A strong aerobic system:

  • Provides energy for low-to-moderate intensity movement (70% of the game)
  • Enables faster recovery between high-intensity efforts
  • Clears lactate more efficiently
  • Supports overall training tolerance

Engine 2: The Anaerobic System

This powers your sprints, tackles, jumps, and explosive changes of direction:

  • Fuels high-intensity efforts (30% of actions, but most decisive)
  • Determines sprint speed and repeated sprint ability
  • Powers accelerations and decelerations

Elite soccer players have developed both engines to a high level. They can sustain quality movement for 90 minutes AND produce decisive explosive actions when needed.

Building the Aerobic Foundation

Zone 2 Training

The foundation. Professional players accumulate significant Zone 2 volume, especially in pre-season.

Zone 2 Guidelines for Soccer

  • Pre-season: 3-4 sessions per week, 40-60 minutes
  • In-season: 1-2 sessions per week for maintenance
  • Modality: Running preferred for specificity, but cycling/swimming reduce load
  • Heart rate: 65-75% max, conversational pace

Long Runs

One longer session (45-60+ minutes) per week builds the aerobic ceiling. Run at a steady, conversational pace — this isn't a race.

Developing Repeated Sprint Ability (RSA)

RSA is the capacity to maintain sprint performance over multiple efforts with incomplete recovery. This is the specific fitness that wins games.

RSA Training Protocols

Protocol 1: Classic RSA

  • 6x40m sprints
  • 25 seconds recovery (jog back)
  • 3-4 sets
  • 3-4 minutes between sets

Goal: Maintain sprint times across all reps. When speed drops >5%, reduce volume.

Protocol 2: Game-Simulating Intervals

  • 15 sec sprint
  • 15 sec jog
  • Continue for 4 minutes
  • 3 min rest
  • Repeat 3-4 times

Mimics the intermittent nature of soccer match play.

High-Intensity Interval Training

Develop VO2 max and the ability to work at high intensities:

  • 4x4 Protocol: 4 min at 90-95% max HR, 3 min recovery, 4 sets
  • 30-30s: 30 sec hard (90%+), 30 sec easy, 12-15 min continuous
  • Yo-Yo Intervals: Progressive running test format as training

Small-Sided Games as Conditioning

The best soccer conditioning often happens with a ball at your feet. Small-sided games (SSGs) provide:

  • Sport-specific movement patterns
  • Technical and tactical training combined with conditioning
  • Varied intensities that mimic real game demands
  • Mental engagement that pure running lacks

SSG Conditioning Guidelines

Format Intensity Primary Focus
3v3 (small field) Very High Anaerobic, technical under fatigue
5v5 (medium field) High Mixed, game-like demands
8v8 (large field) Moderate-High Aerobic with sprints
11v11 Match-specific Position-specific demands

Position-Specific Demands

Central Midfielders

Highest total distance (12-14 km), most touches on ball:

  • Emphasis on aerobic endurance
  • Moderate sprint demands but constant movement
  • Need to sustain decision-making under fatigue

Wingers/Wide Players

Highest sprint volume, most high-intensity running:

  • Priority on repeated sprint ability
  • Acceleration and top-speed development
  • 1v1 explosiveness

Central Defenders

Lower total distance, shorter high-intensity bursts:

  • Short sprint capability
  • Heading and jumping power
  • Recovery runs after pressing

Fullbacks

Hybrid demands — significant endurance plus sprint requirements:

  • Long repeated runs up and down the wing
  • Need both engines developed highly
  • Recovery capacity is critical

Sample Pre-Season Training Week

Building the 90-Minute Engine

  • Monday: Zone 2 run (45 min) + strength training
  • Tuesday: Technical training + 4x4 intervals
  • Wednesday: Small-sided games (high intensity) + core work
  • Thursday: Active recovery — light jog, mobility
  • Friday: RSA training + tactical work
  • Saturday: Long run (50-60 min Zone 2)
  • Sunday: Rest

In-Season Management

With 1-2 games per week, conditioning work must be strategic:

Weekly Rhythm (1 Game/Week)

  • Game Day -2: High-intensity training (if early in week), tactical prep
  • Game Day -1: Technical work, set pieces, activation — NO conditioning
  • Game Day: Match
  • Game Day +1: Recovery — pool, bike, light movement for starters; conditioning for non-players
  • Game Day +2: Return to training — moderate intensity
  • Game Day +3: Highest training load day of the week

Testing and Monitoring

Key Fitness Tests for Soccer

  • Yo-Yo Intermittent Recovery Test: Gold standard for soccer endurance
  • 30-15 IFT: Determines max aerobic speed for interval prescription
  • Sprint Tests: 10m, 20m, 40m for acceleration and speed
  • RSA Test: Multiple sprints with short recovery
  • GPS Load Monitoring: In-season tracking of training load

Common Mistakes

  • Too much slow distance: Soccer isn't marathon running. Include high-intensity work.
  • All sprints, no base: Without aerobic foundation, repeated sprints break down quickly.
  • Neglecting recovery: With 50+ games per season, recovery is training.
  • Generic conditioning: Position-specific demands vary. Train accordingly.
  • Ignoring strength: Strength underpins speed, power, and injury resilience.

Strength for Soccer Endurance

Strength training supports conditioning by:

  • Improving running economy (less energy per stride)
  • Reducing injury risk in fatigued states
  • Maintaining power output late in games

Focus on: Squats, deadlifts, single-leg work, Nordic hamstring curls, core stability. 2x per week in-season is sufficient for maintenance.

Bottom Line

Soccer conditioning is about building two engines — the aerobic base that sustains 90 minutes of work, and the anaerobic system that produces game-changing explosive actions. Neglect either, and your performance will suffer.

Build your aerobic foundation with Zone 2 work and long intervals. Develop repeated sprint ability with sport-specific protocols. Use small-sided games to train conditioning with the ball. Manage load in-season to stay fresh when it matters.

The goal is simple: be as fast and powerful in minute 89 as you were in minute 1. That's the 90-minute engine.
