Performance Anxiety: Managing Competition Nerves

Why anxiety exists, when it helps, and practical strategies for channeling nervous energy into performance.

Mental Anxiety Competition

Understanding Performance Anxiety

Your heart pounds. Palms sweat. Stomach churns. Thoughts race. It's the night before competition, or the moments before a crucial performance, and your body is telling you something is wrong. Except it isn't wrong — it's normal. And it's potentially useful.

Performance anxiety is the body's natural response to perceived threat or challenge. The problem isn't the anxiety itself — it's how we interpret and respond to it.

The Reframe

Anxiety and excitement produce nearly identical physiological responses. The difference is psychological interpretation. Top performers learn to interpret pre-competition arousal as excitement and readiness rather than fear and threat.

The Physiology of Anxiety

The Stress Response

When you perceive a challenge or threat, your body activates the sympathetic nervous system:

  • Adrenaline surge: Heart rate increases, blood pressure rises
  • Blood redistribution: Flow to muscles increases, digestion shuts down
  • Heightened alertness: Senses sharpen, attention narrows
  • Energy mobilization: Glucose released into bloodstream

These responses evolved to help you survive physical threats. They also help athletic performance — when channeled correctly.

The Yerkes-Dodson Law

There's an optimal level of arousal for performance:

Arousal-Performance Relationship

Arousal Level Effect on Performance
Too low Flat, unfocused, slow reactions
Optimal Alert, focused, ready — peak performance
Too high Tight, overthinking, choking

The goal isn't to eliminate anxiety — it's to optimize arousal level for your performance.

Types of Competitive Anxiety

Cognitive Anxiety

The mental component — worry, negative thoughts, self-doubt:

  • "What if I fail?"
  • "Everyone is watching."
  • "I can't mess this up."
  • "I'm not ready."

Somatic Anxiety

The physical symptoms:

  • Increased heart rate
  • Muscle tension
  • Sweating
  • Nausea, "butterflies"
  • Rapid breathing

Effective anxiety management addresses both components with different strategies.

Strategies for Cognitive Anxiety

1. Reframe the Meaning

Research by Harvard psychologist Alison Wood Brooks found that simply reframing anxiety as excitement improved performance on tests, singing, and public speaking.

Practice: When you feel anxious, say "I'm excited" rather than "I'm nervous." Your body doesn't know the difference — but your mind does.

2. Focus on Process, Not Outcome

Anxiety often stems from outcome focus — winning, not losing, looking good. Shift to process:

  • What specific actions will you execute?
  • What's your technique focus?
  • What's within your control right now?

3. Thought Stopping and Replacement

Interrupt negative thought patterns:

  1. Notice the negative thought
  2. Say "STOP" internally (some athletes use a physical cue like snapping a rubber band)
  3. Replace with a prepared positive or neutral statement

Replacement Statements

  • "I've trained for this."
  • "Focus on the process."
  • "This is just another performance."
  • "I've done this a thousand times."
  • "Breathe. Execute. Trust."

4. Pre-Performance Routines

Consistent routines create predictability in unpredictable situations:

  • Same warm-up sequence
  • Same pre-performance ritual
  • Same music, visualization, breathing pattern

Strategies for Somatic Anxiety

1. Controlled Breathing

The fastest way to regulate the nervous system:

Box Breathing Protocol

  1. Inhale for 4 counts
  2. Hold for 4 counts
  3. Exhale for 4 counts
  4. Hold for 4 counts
  5. Repeat 4-6 cycles

This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces physical anxiety symptoms.

2. Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Systematically tense and release muscle groups to reduce overall tension:

  • Tense each muscle group for 5-10 seconds
  • Release completely for 20-30 seconds
  • Progress from feet to face
  • Notice the contrast between tension and relaxation

3. Centering

A quick technique used by elite athletes:

  1. Take a deep breath from your diaphragm
  2. Focus on your center of gravity (just below the navel)
  3. Exhale, consciously releasing tension
  4. Direct attention to a specific performance cue

4. Physical Movement

Sometimes the best way to manage physical anxiety is physical activity:

  • Walk around, don't sit still
  • Light dynamic movement
  • Shake out tension
  • Use your warm-up to burn off excess energy

Long-Term Anxiety Management

Simulation Training

Practice under conditions that simulate competition:

  • Add consequences to training (small bets, public commitment)
  • Practice with observers present
  • Use visualization to mentally rehearse competition scenarios
  • Compete frequently to normalize the experience

Build Robust Confidence

True confidence comes from preparation:

  • Train thoroughly — know you've done the work
  • Keep a success log — evidence of competence
  • Recall past successful performances
  • Use accomplishment-focused self-talk

Develop Acceptance

Some anxiety is inevitable. Acceptance reduces its power:

  • "I feel anxious, and that's okay."
  • "This feeling means I care."
  • "I can perform well even with nerves."

Pre-Competition Protocol

The Hour Before

  1. 60 min: Begin warm-up routine, stay physically active
  2. 45 min: Brief visualization — see successful execution
  3. 30 min: If anxious, use breathing/centering techniques
  4. 15 min: Specific warm-up for your event
  5. 5 min: Final centering, cue words, performance focus
  6. Start: Trust training, execute

When Anxiety Becomes a Problem

Some anxiety is normal and even helpful. Seek professional support if:

  • Anxiety is preventing you from competing or training
  • Performance significantly declines despite good preparation
  • Anxiety extends beyond sport into daily life
  • Physical symptoms are severe or debilitating
  • Self-help strategies aren't working

Sport psychologists specialize in performance anxiety and can provide targeted interventions.

Bottom Line

Performance anxiety is not your enemy — it's your body preparing for action. The goal isn't to eliminate nervousness, but to interpret it productively and manage its intensity. With practice, the same physiological state that once felt like fear can feel like excitement and readiness.

Develop your toolkit: breathing techniques for physical symptoms, cognitive strategies for worried thoughts, and consistent routines that create familiarity in high-pressure situations. The butterflies will always be there — your job is to get them flying in formation.
