Blood Biomarkers for Athletes: What to Test and Why

Your blood tells a story. Understanding key biomarkers can reveal hidden limiters to performance and recovery.

Biomarkers Health Optimization

Why Blood Testing Matters

You can train perfectly and eat well but still underperform if underlying deficiencies or imbalances exist. Blood work reveals what subjective feelings and external metrics can't see — the internal environment that supports (or limits) your performance.

Regular blood testing helps you:

  • Identify deficiencies before they become problems
  • Optimize markers for performance, not just "normal" health
  • Monitor training stress and recovery status
  • Make informed decisions about supplementation
  • Track long-term health alongside performance

The Athlete's Standard

"Normal" lab ranges are designed to identify disease, not optimize performance. Athletes often need to aim for optimal ranges rather than just avoiding deficiency.

Key Biomarkers for Athletes

Iron Status Panel

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency in athletes, especially:

  • Endurance athletes (foot strike hemolysis, sweat losses)
  • Female athletes (menstrual losses)
  • Vegetarian/vegan athletes

Iron Panel Components

Marker Purpose Optimal Range
Ferritin Iron stores 50-150 ng/mL for athletes
Hemoglobin Oxygen-carrying capacity M: 14-17.5 g/dL; F: 12-15.5 g/dL
Serum Iron Circulating iron 60-170 mcg/dL
Transferrin Saturation Iron transport capacity 20-45%

Low ferritin (<30) can impair performance even with "normal" hemoglobin.

Vitamin D

Vitamin D influences:

  • Muscle function and strength
  • Bone health and stress fracture risk
  • Immune function
  • Testosterone production
  • Mood and recovery

Test: 25-hydroxyvitamin D
Optimal: 40-60 ng/mL (many athletes deficient, especially in winter or indoor sports)

Thyroid Function

The thyroid regulates metabolism, energy, and recovery. Athletes, especially endurance athletes in caloric deficit, can develop subclinical hypothyroidism.

Thyroid Panel

  • TSH: 0.5-2.5 mIU/L (optimal)
  • Free T3: 3.0-4.0 pg/mL
  • Free T4: 1.0-1.5 ng/dL

High training volume + caloric restriction can suppress thyroid function.

Hormones

Testosterone (Men)

Total testosterone should be 500-900 ng/dL for optimal performance. Values below 400 may impair recovery and adaptation. Also test free testosterone and SHBG for fuller picture.

Cortisol

The stress hormone. Chronically elevated cortisol suggests overtraining or life stress. Morning cortisol should be 10-20 mcg/dL; chronically elevated or suppressed values are concerning.

Inflammation Markers

C-Reactive Protein (hs-CRP)

General inflammation marker. Optimal: <1.0 mg/L. Chronically elevated values suggest systemic inflammation that can impair recovery.

Homocysteine

Inflammation and methylation marker. Optimal: <10 μmol/L. Elevated levels associated with cardiovascular risk and possible B-vitamin deficiencies.

Metabolic Markers

Key Metabolic Tests

  • Fasting Glucose: 70-90 mg/dL (optimal)
  • Fasting Insulin: <5 μIU/mL (optimal)
  • HbA1c: <5.3% (optimal)
  • Triglycerides: <100 mg/dL
  • HDL: >50 mg/dL (men), >60 mg/dL (women)

Metabolic health underlies long-term performance and disease prevention.

Other Important Markers

  • Vitamin B12: 500-900 pg/mL (especially for plant-based athletes)
  • Folate: >10 ng/mL
  • Magnesium (RBC): 5.0-6.5 mg/dL (serum magnesium is less informative)
  • Creatine Kinase (CK): Muscle damage marker; useful for monitoring recovery

Testing Frequency

Recommended Schedule

  • Comprehensive panel: 2x per year (off-season and pre-competition)
  • Follow-up on issues: 6-12 weeks after interventions
  • Iron (at-risk athletes): Every 3-4 months
  • Vitamin D: 2x per year (end of summer, end of winter)

Interpreting Results

Normal vs. Optimal

"Normal" ranges are based on population averages, which include unhealthy people. Athletes should target optimal ranges, not just avoid deficiency.

Context Matters

  • Test in consistent conditions (fasted, same time of day)
  • Avoid testing immediately after hard training (inflammation, muscle damage)
  • Consider menstrual cycle timing for female athletes
  • Compare to YOUR baseline, not just population norms

Single Values vs. Trends

One abnormal value isn't necessarily concerning. Track over time to identify meaningful patterns.

How to Get Tested

Options

  • Primary care physician: Insurance may cover basic panels
  • Sports medicine doctor: Better understanding of athlete-specific needs
  • Direct-to-consumer services: InsideTracker, Marek Health, etc.
  • Quest/Labcorp self-pay: Often more affordable than through providers

Basic Panel for Athletes

Recommended Starting Tests

  1. Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  2. Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)
  3. Iron panel (ferritin, serum iron, TIBC)
  4. Vitamin D
  5. Lipid panel
  6. Thyroid (TSH, free T3, free T4)
  7. hs-CRP

Add hormones, homocysteine, and B vitamins for more comprehensive picture.

Common Findings in Athletes

Low Ferritin

Extremely common, especially in runners and female athletes. Supplement under guidance — too much iron is harmful.

Low Vitamin D

Most athletes are deficient, especially in winter or if training indoors. Supplement 2,000-5,000 IU daily, retest in 3 months.

Suppressed Testosterone

Can occur with overtraining, undereating, or excessive endurance training. Address root causes first.

Elevated Inflammation

Chronic training stress, poor sleep, or dietary issues can elevate hs-CRP. Often responds to lifestyle intervention.

Working with Results

  1. Prioritize: Address deficiencies before optimizations
  2. Food first: Improve through diet before supplementation when possible
  3. Targeted supplementation: Based on actual deficiency, not guessing
  4. Retest: Confirm interventions are working
  5. Work with professionals: Interpretation benefits from expertise

Bottom Line

Blood biomarkers offer a window into your body's internal environment that no training log or wearable can provide. Understanding your iron status, vitamin D, thyroid function, and metabolic markers can reveal hidden limiters to performance and guide targeted interventions.

Get a baseline panel, establish your individual norms, address deficiencies, and track over time. The data can transform your approach from guessing to knowing.
